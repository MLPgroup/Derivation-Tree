Abstract

We study the problem of a particle hopping on the Bethe lattice in the presence of a Coulomb potential. We obtain an exact solution to the particle’s Green’s function along with the full energy spectrum. In addition, we present a mapping of a generalized radial potential problem defined on the Bethe lattice to an infinite number of one dimensional problems that are easily accessible numerically. The latter method is particularly useful when the problem admits no analytical solution.

I Introduction

In many body physics, exactly solvable models are few and far between. In their absence, a common strategy to pursue is the use of mean field approximations Weiss (1907), where the interactions between a finite number of the system’s constituents and the rest are modeled through an effective field approximating the effects of the latter on the former. One of such approaches bears the name of its inventor, Hans Bethe Bethe (1935), and turns out to be exact on cycle-free graphs Kurata et al. (1953). This coined the term “Bethe lattice”, which, despite its seeming unphysicalness, has since been successfully used to describe a plethora of physical phenomena, including excitations in antiferromagnets Brinkman and Rice (1970), Anderson localization Abou-Chacra et al. (1973), percolation Fisher and Essam (1961), and hopping of ions in ice Chen et al. (1974) to name a few. In particular, the Coulomb potential problem on the Bethe lattice, first appearing in Gallinar (1984) and addressed in this work, recently surfaced in a study of quantum spin ice by the authors Petrova et al. (2015).

In this paper we treat the problem of a single particle hopping on the Bethe lattice in the presence of a radial potential:

H=T+V⁢(n)𝐻𝑇𝑉𝑛italic-H RELOP:equals italic-T ADDOP:plus italic-V OPEN:( italic-n CLOSE:)H=T+V(n) (1)

where T𝑇italic-TT is -t𝑡ADDOP:minus italic-t-t times the adjacency matrix, and the potential V⁢(n)𝑉𝑛italic-V OPEN:( italic-n CLOSE:)V(n) is a function of the Bethe lattice generation n𝑛italic-nn only – i.e., a radial potential. The two central results of this work are: (a) a mapping of the general radial V⁢(n)𝑉𝑛italic-V OPEN:( italic-n CLOSE:)V(n) problem to a family of one dimensional chains, and (b) the exact solution for the attractive Coulomb potential, V⁢(n)=Cn𝑉𝑛𝐶𝑛italic-V OPEN:( italic-n CLOSE:) RELOP:equals FRACOP:italic-divide ARG:start italic-C ARG:end ARG:start italic-n ARG:endV(n)=\frac{C}{n} where C<0𝐶0italic-C RELOP:less-than NUMBER:0C<0. The latter comes in the form of a closed form expression for the lattice Green’s function. With that, one can obtain the energy levels of the model, and the local density of states as a function of n𝑛italic-nn.

The mapping of the Bethe lattice model (1) to a family of 1⁢d1𝑑NUMBER:1 italic-d1d problems, is particularly useful when the exact solution to the problem cannot be obtained. Normally, numerical treatments of Bethe lattice models are of limited use owing to the large fraction (no less than half the total) of vertices at the edges. Mapping to 1⁢d1𝑑NUMBER:1 italic-d1d chains lets us circumvent this obstacle.

The paper is structured as follows. In Section II we discuss some features common to all models where a single particle is hopping on the Bethe lattice, or a finite Cayley tree, in the presence of a radial potential: the symmetries of the Hamiltonian (1) (II.1), the mapping of different symmetry sectors to a family of 1⁢d1𝑑NUMBER:1 italic-d1d problems (II.2), and the continued fraction technique to solving Eq. (1) perturbatively (II.3). In the technical heart of the paper, Section III, we present the exact solution to the Coulomb potential problem on the Bethe lattice, obtained by carrying out the aforementioned perturbative calculation to infinite order. We summarize our findings and discuss possible applications in Section IV.

II.1 Symmetries of the model

Here and in Section II.2, we treat the z=3𝑧3italic-z RELOP:equals NUMBER:3z=3 case for concreteness unless stated otherwise. We refer to two vertices at generation (r+1)𝑟1OPEN:( italic-r ADDOP:plus NUMBER:1 CLOSE:)(r+1) as siblings if they are connected to the same parent vertex at generation r𝑟italic-rr.

Exchanging left and right siblings at a given generation leaves the Hamiltonian (1) invariant Chen et al. (1974). These operations commute, which allows us to associate a separate quantum number, denoting the parity under such exchanges, with each generation n>1𝑛1italic-n RELOP:greater-than NUMBER:1n>1. Each such symmetry sector can be mapped onto a 1⁢d1𝑑NUMBER:1 italic-d1d half-line, whose origin is offset by the number equal to the highest Bethe lattice generation with an odd quantum number for a given sector.

To understand the physical meaning of the mapping to follow, consider three vertices depicted in Fig. 1(b): parent site 1 at generation r𝑟italic-rr, and siblings 2 and 3 at generation r+1𝑟1italic-r ADDOP:plus NUMBER:1r+1 that are connected to it. The connection between the spectra of a Hamiltonian defined on a half-line and a related problem on the Bethe lattice was known previously Aizenman et al. (2006). IV Summary and Discussion

We have obtained an exact solution for the spectrum of the Coulomb potential problem on the (K+1)𝐾1OPEN:( italic-K ADDOP:plus NUMBER:1 CLOSE:)(K+1)-coordinated Bethe lattice. The energy levels of different symmetry sectors are given by the poles of the forward hopping Green’s functions (9). The sectors correspond to a choice of even or one of the (K-1)𝐾1OPEN:( italic-K ADDOP:minus NUMBER:1 CLOSE:)(K-1) odd quantum numbers for each generation of the Bethe lattice n>1𝑛1italic-n RELOP:greater-than NUMBER:1n>1. If the highest generation with an odd quantum number is k𝑘italic-kk, then all of the states in the corresponding sectors have zero amplitude at nodes belonging to generations n<k𝑛𝑘italic-n RELOP:less-than italic-kn<k, and the sectors’ energy levels, obtained from the poles of GkF⁢(ω)superscriptsubscript𝐺𝑘𝐹𝜔italic-G POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-k POSTSUBSCRIPT:end POSTSUPERSCRIPT:start italic-F POSTSUPERSCRIPT:end OPEN:( italic-omega CLOSE:)G_{k}^{F}(\omega), are Kk-2superscript𝐾𝑘2italic-K POSTSUPERSCRIPT:start italic-k ADDOP:minus NUMBER:2 POSTSUPERSCRIPT:endK^{k-2}-fold degenerate. Each such sector can be mapped onto a hopping problem on an infinite half-line, where V⁢(n)𝑉𝑛italic-V OPEN:( italic-n CLOSE:)V(n) is offset by k𝑘italic-kk and the hopping amplitude is multiplied by K𝐾italic-square-root ARG:start italic-K ARG:end\sqrt{K}. This mapping exists for any problem whose Hamiltonian has the form (1), and is particularly useful when, unlike in the Coulomb potential case, an exact solution cannot be obtained.

Models which involve approximating various physical structures as well as mathematical constructions by cycle-free graphs are plentiful. A single particle hopping on such graph can capture complex many body physics. For instance, the motion of a free particle on the Bethe lattice has been used to study antiferromagnets Brinkman and Rice (1970) and water ice Chen et al. (1974), whereas a particle hopping in the presence of random and radial potentials was used to model Anderson localization Abou-Chacra et al. (1973) and spin ice Petrova et al. (2015) respectively.

Apart from the few known exactly solvable cases, problems defined on Cayley trees are difficult to treat. While the exact diagonalizaion of a single particle problem may seem manageable using numerical methods, the large number of nodes at the boundary poses an immense difficulty. One may circumvent the issue by introducing closed cycles at the ends and/or focusing on the properties associated with the interior of the tree. We demonstrate that there is an alternative point of view for radial potentials. The mapping of each sector onto a one-dimensional problem allows for numerical treatment with tractable boundary effects stemming from the single edge node.

In addition to the broadly applicable mapping above, the exact solution derived in the present work may prove to be of use across various fields of physics, the inverse r𝑟italic-rr potentials being among the most common. In fact, the motivation behind this study came from considering the problem of emergent magnetic monopoles arising in a particular class of frustrated magnets – spin ice – where the Bethe lattice turns out to be a good approximation for the state space of the problem, and the Coulomb potential defined on it served to represent interactions between pairs of monopoles Petrova et al. (2015).
