Supercurrent Vortex Pinball via a Triplet Cooper Pair Inverse Edelstein Effect

Morten Amundsen and Jacob Linder Department of Physics, Norwegian University of Science and Technology, N-7491 Trondheim, Norway
July 10, 2019
Abstract

We consider the Josephson effect through a thin spin-orbit coupled layer in the presence of an exchange field 𝒉𝒉bold-italic-h\bm{h}, and discover a set of supercurrent vortices appearing in the system which can be controllably moved around in the system by varying either the direction of 𝒉𝒉bold-italic-h\bm{h}, the strength |𝒉|𝒉VERTBAR:| bold-italic-h VERTBAR:||\bm{h}|, or the spin-orbit coupling magnitude α𝛼italic-alpha\alpha via a gate voltage. We refer to this phenomenon as a supercurrent vortex pinball effect and show that its origin is the spin polarization of the triplet Cooper pairs induced in the system. The supercurrent vortices thus arise from what resembles a Cooper pair-induced inverse Edelstein effect. Our results highlight the importance of considering higher-dimensional models for superconducting hybrid structures which unveils novel phenomena that are hidden in commonly used effective 1D models.

The proximity effect endows otherwise non-superconducting materials with superconducting properties, and thus offers an interesting arena to explore how quantum coherence is manifested in unusual physical environments. One of the most actively pursued setups includes so-called Josephson junctions where a material is able to sustain a supercurrent via proximity to two host superconductors. In the presence of a magnetic exchange field 𝒉𝒉bold-italic-h\bm{h}, the Cooper pairs that leak from the superconductors oscillate between the conventional singlet spin state and the opposite-spin triplet state buzdin_rmp_05 . If the exchange field is spatially varying, or the system contains spin-orbit coupling, the triplet Cooper pairs may rotate between different triplet states where the electron spins are parallel, making tunable superconducting spin transport possible bergeret_prl_01 ; linder_nphys_15 ; eschrig_rpp_15 . In light of this discovery, the effect of spin-orbit coupling on proximitized materials has recently been investigated in several works bergeret_prl_13 ; jacobsen_prb_2015 ; jacobsen_prb_2015b ; konschelle_prb_15 ; arjoranta_prb_16 ; alidoust_njp_15 ; pershoguba_prl_15 ; konschelle_prb_2016 ; costa_arxiv_16 ; bobkova_arxiv_16 ; takashima_prb_16 ; jacobsen_scirep_15 ; yu_prb_16 ; tkachov_prl_17 . However, due to the complexity of the resulting equations, a common simplification is to reduce the system considered to effective 1D numerical models. This has the disadvantage that higher dimensional phenomena cannot be explored. In this Letter, we do not make use of such an approximation, and instead consider a thin film of a non-superconducting material with spin-orbit coupling sandwiched between two superconductors, thereby creating a 2D Josephson junction (lateral geometry). This system is found to display novel, inherently two-dimensional, physical phenomena which are hidden in effective one-dimensional models. We discover a set of supercurrent vortices appearing in the system which can be controllably moved around in the system by varying either the direction of 𝒉𝒉bold-italic-h\bm{h}, the strength |𝒉|𝒉VERTBAR:| bold-italic-h VERTBAR:||\bm{h}|, or the spin-orbit coupling magnitude α𝛼italic-alpha\alpha via a gate voltage. We refer to this phenomenon as a supercurrent vortex pinball effect and show that its origin is the spin polarization of the triplet Cooper pairs induced in the system. The supercurrent vortices thus arise from what is reminiscent of a Cooper pair-induced inverse Edelstein effect.

In other words we find that, due to spin-orbit coupling, the existence of a finite triplet Cooper pair spin expectation value directly produces a charge current, which we interpret as a triplet Cooper pair induced inverse Edelstein effect. We note that another type of Edelstein effect in superconducting hybrid structures has recently been reported in Ref. konschelle_prb_15 , where a spontaneous supercurrent induced by magnetization in a Josephson junction with spin-orbit coupling was found. Here, we have presented a different inverse Edelstein effect, in that the spin density responsible for the current is solely generated by the triplet Cooper pairs. This is in contrast to the induced magnetization which requires a non-zero singlet contribution. Recently, the nonequilibrium Edelstein effect and magnetoelectric Andreev transport was discussed in the context of helical metals tkachov_prl_17 .

For the system shown in Fig. 1, we solve the full Usadel equation, given in Eq. 1, by using the finite element method, as thoroughly explained in Ref. amundsen_scirep_16 . The first thing to note is that the current density has a non-trivial transversal distribution, and even changes sign in certain areas. In the regions where a sign change occurs, supercurrent vortices are generated, i.e. positions around which the current density circulates. Vortices in the current density have been reported in proximitized materials in the presence of an external magnetic flux cuevas_prl_07 ; bergeret_jlt_08 ; alidoust_prl_12 ; amundsen_scirep_16 , whereas no such flux is required in the present work. These flux-induced vortices are associated with a suppression of superconducting correlations at precisely the location of the vortices in addition to a phase-winding of the superconducting phase, analogously to Abrikosov vortices. In the results presented here, we do not find any such suppression, and so the spin-orbit induced supercurrent vortices are therefore of a different nature.

The effect is clearly seen in Fig. (a)a, in which the exchange field is pointing in the x𝑥italic-xx-direction. Here, the current density flows in the positive x𝑥italic-xx-direction near the center of the junction, and in the opposite direction by the edges. This creates a circulation around two oblong vortices at approximately y=±0.4⁢L𝑦plus-or-minus0.4𝐿italic-y RELOP:equals ADDOP:plus-or-minus NUMBER:0.4 italic-Ly=\pm 0.4L within which the current density is suppressed. Such a current distribution may be measured experimentally using magneto-optic imaging johansen_prb_96 .

Fig. (b)b shows the transverse distribution of the current flowing across the junction. Specifically, it shows the different contributions in Eq. 4 contribute to the total current. It is seen that the transverse distribution of the individual components Jksubscript𝐽𝑘italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-k POSTSUBSCRIPT:endJ_{k}—the components not explicitly dependent on α𝛼italic-alpha\alpha—almost perfectly cancel, rendering their sum JIsubscript𝐽𝐼italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-I POSTSUBSCRIPT:endJ_{I} constant. It is therefore clear that the main contribution to the transverse distribution stems from Jt⁢tsubscript𝐽𝑡𝑡italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-t italic-t POSTSUBSCRIPT:endJ_{tt}, the term responsible for the inverse Edelstein effect induced by the triplet Cooper pair spins.

A key point is that the existence of the vortices are found to depend on the strength h=|𝒉|ℎ𝒉italic-h RELOP:equals VERTBAR:| bold-italic-h VERTBAR:|h=|\bm{h}| of the exchange field. When examining the current passing between the superconductors for increasing exchange field, a decaying oscillatory pattern is found where, for certain values of hℎitalic-hh, the current flows in the opposite direction. This is known as a π𝜋italic-pi\pi-junction ryazanov_prl_01 , owing to the fact that the ground state of the Josephson junction has a phase difference of π𝜋italic-pi\pi rather than zero buzdin_rmp_05 . We find that the vortices are harbingers of a 0-π𝜋italic-pi\pi transition, being present only when the total current is significantly reduced. The reason for this is that the transversal distribution of the current density is much less influenced by the strength of the exchange field than the current itself. For increasing hℎitalic-hh, the curvature of Jt⁢tsubscript𝐽𝑡𝑡italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-t italic-t POSTSUBSCRIPT:endJ_{tt} in Fig. (b)b is more or less retained, while the total current—that is, the average of the current density—is reduced upon approaching a 0-π𝜋italic-pi\pi transition. Close enough to the transition, the minimas of the current density will cross zero and become negative. Evidently, a current density redistribution is less energetically favorable than vortex generation.

It turns out that for an in-plane exchange field, the current is reduced rapidly enough with increasing hℎitalic-hh for vortices to remain present once they first appear. This can be seen in Fig. 3, which shows the total current I𝐼italic-II, found by integrating the current density 𝑱𝑱bold-italic-J\bm{J} over the cross section. The exchange field is applied at an angle θ=45∘𝜃superscript45italic-theta RELOP:equals NUMBER:45 POSTSUPERSCRIPT:start MULOP:compose POSTSUPERSCRIPT:end\theta=45^{\circ} with respect to the y𝑦italic-yy-axis. In the pink region there are vortices in the current density, and it is observed that the modulation of the curve hinders a reentry into the green region, where vortices are not present. For an out-of-plane exchange field this is not necessarily the case, and away from the transition points, the current may become large enough for the vortices to disappear.

Interestingly, the location of the vortices also changes as the exchange field is increased. The insets of Fig. 3 show the current density distribution for selected points along the current curve. The vortices first appear at the vacuum edges, on opposite sides of the junction, near the superconducting interfaces. As hℎitalic-hh is increased, they translate vertically and pass the x𝑥italic-xx-axis at precisely the 0-π𝜋italic-pi\pi transition. At this stage, the current density distribution is symmetric about both the x𝑥italic-xx and the y𝑦italic-yy axis, with no net current passing between the superconductors. Further increase of hℎitalic-hh causes further translation of the vortices. However, since they must cross the x𝑥italic-xx-axis every 0-π𝜋italic-pi\pi transition due to the symmetry requirements, a turning point must be reached, and the motion of the vortices may best be described as resembling a damped harmonic oscillator.

In the study of a 1D Josephson junction with spin-orbit coupling, it was recently discovered that the critical current varies greatly with the direction of an applied exchange field, even creating 0-π𝜋italic-pi\pi transitions arjoranta_prb_16 ; costa_arxiv_16 . It is therefore reasonable to presume that the current density distribution becomes non-trivial, although the abovementioned previous works could not determine this due to their simplified 1D models. We find that this is indeed the case. With an exchange field of strength h=1.0⁢Δℎ1.0Δitalic-h RELOP:equals NUMBER:1.0 Deltah=1.0\Delta pointing in the transversal direction (θ=0∘𝜃superscript0italic-theta RELOP:equals NUMBER:0 POSTSUPERSCRIPT:start MULOP:compose POSTSUPERSCRIPT:end\theta=0^{\circ}), no vortices are found. Rotating 𝒉𝒉bold-italic-h\bm{h} increases the transversal variation of the current density, and at around θ=30∘𝜃superscript30italic-theta RELOP:equals NUMBER:30 POSTSUPERSCRIPT:start MULOP:compose POSTSUPERSCRIPT:end\theta=30^{\circ} vortices appear, as shown in Fig. (a)a. Further rotation translates the vortices vertically towards the x𝑥italic-xx-axis, as seen in Fig. (b)b for θ=60∘𝜃superscript60italic-theta RELOP:equals NUMBER:60 POSTSUPERSCRIPT:start MULOP:compose POSTSUPERSCRIPT:end\theta=60^{\circ}, before translating towards y𝑦italic-yy-axis, and ending up like Fig. (a)a for θ=90∘𝜃superscript90italic-theta RELOP:equals NUMBER:90 POSTSUPERSCRIPT:start MULOP:compose POSTSUPERSCRIPT:end\theta=90^{\circ}.

Finally, we also determine how the strength of the spin-orbit coupling affects the vortices. Tuning of the Rashba parameter α𝛼italic-alpha\alpha can be achieved experimentally in a 2DEG by means of a gate voltage nitta_prl_97 ; engels_prb_97 ; grundler_prl_00 . For small spin-orbit coupling strength, so too is the contribution from Jt⁢tsubscript𝐽𝑡𝑡italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-t italic-t POSTSUBSCRIPT:endJ_{tt}, as can be seen from the explicit dependence on α𝛼italic-alpha\alpha in Eq. 6. With increasing α𝛼italic-alpha\alpha, the inverse Cooper pair Edelstein effect predicted here increases both in terms of the curvature and the amplitude of Jt⁢tsubscript𝐽𝑡𝑡italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-t italic-t POSTSUBSCRIPT:endJ_{tt}, making the existence of supercurrent vortices possible. Since varying α𝛼italic-alpha\alpha changes the topography of Jt⁢tsubscript𝐽𝑡𝑡italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-t italic-t POSTSUBSCRIPT:endJ_{tt}, it is reasonable that the vortex locations also changes. This can be seen in Figs. (c)c and (d)d, which show the current density distribution for α⁢L=1.2𝛼𝐿1.2italic-alpha italic-L RELOP:equals NUMBER:1.2\alpha L=1.2 and α⁢L=1.6𝛼𝐿1.6italic-alpha italic-L RELOP:equals NUMBER:1.6\alpha L=1.6, respectively. It is noted that for large enough Rashba parameter α𝛼italic-alpha\alpha, no 0-π𝜋italic-pi\pi transition will take place arjoranta_prb_16 , and vortices will only appear for large exchange fields where the conventional contribution to the current, JIsubscript𝐽𝐼italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-I POSTSUBSCRIPT:endJ_{I}, is sufficiently suppressed relative to Jt⁢tsubscript𝐽𝑡𝑡italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-t italic-t POSTSUBSCRIPT:endJ_{tt}.

Conclusion. We have shown that by performing a 2D analysis, accounting for the transversal dimension, a hidden phenomenon is uncovered when spin-orbit coupling is included in the Josephson junction. We find that vortices appear in the current density, and that their presence and location may be tuned by varying either the exchange field strength, its direction, or the strength of the spin-orbit coupling. This ”supercurrent vortex pinball effect” has its origin in the spin polarization of the triplet Cooper pairs induced in the system, and thus arises from what may be interpreted as a Cooper pair-induced inverse Edelstein effect.
