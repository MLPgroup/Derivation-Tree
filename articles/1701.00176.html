1 Introduction

The lifting of the degeneracy of the energy levels in molecule by a magnetic field is a well-known and well-studied phenomenon. Thus it has spawned experimental techniques such as laser magnetic resonance spectroscopy Davies (1981); Gordon etÂ al. (2006), magnetic rotation spectroscopy McCarthy and Field (1992), and optical Zeeman spectroscopy Zhang and Steimle (2014); Qin etÂ al. (2012). These techniques, for example, use the Zeeman effect to tune transitions in and out resonance by changing the applied magnetic field Chen etÂ al. (2007). Zeeman effects can also be probed directly using standard spectroscopic techniques to study molecules in magnetic fields Vallon etÂ al. (2009); Ross etÂ al. (2012); Crozet etÂ al. (2014). In a similar fashion, Zeeman effects are increasingly being used to form, probe and trap molecules at ultra-cold temperatures Weinstein etÂ al. (1998) for example by use of magnetically tunable Feshbach resonances KÃ¶hler etÂ al. (2006).

Spectral shifts and splittings provide a remote sensing technique with which to study the Universe. Zeeman splitting of molecular spectra are actively being used to probe magnetic fields in a variety of astronomical environments including sunspots Berdyugina etÂ al. (2000); Berdyugina and Solanki (2001); Lozitska etÂ al. (2015), starspots Afram and Berdyugina (2015), white dwarfs Kawka and Vennes (2014); Ferrario etÂ al. (2015) M-dwarfs Shulyak etÂ al. (2010) and potentially exoplanets Reiners etÂ al. (2013).

The Zeeman splitting patterns of the spectrum of an open shell diatomic molecule can be calculated in a straightforward fashion provided that the quantum numbers characterizing states in question are known and are conserved. However, there are circumstances, such as resonance interactions between nearby states via spin-orbit or other couplings where the quantum numbers used to specify the electronic state associated with a given level are not precisely conserved. In this case evaluation of Zeeman splitting as represented by the LandÃ© gğ‘”italic-gg-factor is not straightforward and requires a numerical treatment. It is such a treatment which is the focus of the present article.

The important advantage of the Zeeman effect is that the associated splitting can be made large enough to separate otherwise degenerate spin-components (Î›Î›Lambda\Lambda-doublet). Moreover, the measurement of the gğ‘”italic-gg values can be more accurate than the energy spacing (Gray etÂ al., 1990).

The Zeeman methodology for diatomics was introduced by Schade Schadee (1978). Stolyarov et al. Stolyarov etÂ al. Le Royâ€™s LEVEL Le Roy (2007) has become the program of choice for solving the diatomic nuclear motion problem. For this reason we have written our own diatomic nuclear motion code Duo Yurchenko etÂ al. (2016). Duo explicitly treats open shell systems and can allow for coupling between the various states involved. In this context, of particular interest to us are the many open-shell diatomic molecules which are known to be present, or may be present, in hot astronomical atmospheres such as those found in cool stars and exoplanets. Such species are being studied as part of the ExoMol project Tennyson and Yurchenko (2012). 5 Conclusion

We have developed a numerical procedure for evaluating LandÃ© gğ‘”italic-gg-factors for diatomic molecules without making any assumptions about conserved quantum numbers. This method is tested for four molecules AlO, NO, C22FLOATSUBSCRIPT:start NUMBER:2 FLOATSUBSCRIPT:end{}_{2} and CrH. It would seem that besides making predictions for gğ‘”italic-gg-factors, the comparison between our computed value and the value predicted under the assumption of particular Hundâ€™s case gives a clear means of distinguishing those levels which are best represented by Hundâ€™s case (a) from those which are approximately Hundâ€™s case (b)-like.

The accuracy of our predicted gJsubscriptğ‘”ğ½italic-g POSTSUBSCRIPT:start italic-J POSTSUBSCRIPT:endg_{J} factors depend on a number of factors: (a) the accuracy of the underlying spectroscopic model used and, in particular, its ability to reproduce coupling between different electronic states, (b) our ability to solve this model by, for instance, converging the basis set representation and (c) any assumptions made about angular momentum couplings within the system. Although our procedure is based on a Hundâ€™s case (a) coupling scheme, our general formulation means that no actual approximations are made by adopting a (complete) basis formulated within this scheme. Similarly it is relatively easy, and computationally cheap, to use large vibrational basis sets when converging the problem. This means that the choice of spectroscopic model is likely to be the major source of uncertainty in our calculations or, conversely, that available measurements of LandÃ© gğ‘”italic-gg-factors have the potential to be used to improve the spectroscopic model. In addition we note that our formulation is only appropriate when the changes depend linearly with the magnetic field. Inclusion of non-linear effects require a more sophisticated treatment which we plan to study in future in work.

It is our plan to routinely compute LandÃ© gğ‘”italic-gg-factors for all open shell diatomic species studied as part of the ExoMol project from now on.
