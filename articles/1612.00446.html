1 Introduction

In recent years, scattered light imaging at near-infrared (NIR) wavelengths has revealed intriguing spiral-arm-like features in many protoplanetary disks, such as AB Aur (Hashimoto et al., 2011), MWC 758 (Grady et al., 2013; Benisty et al., 2015), SAO 206462 (Muto et al., 2012; Garufi et al., 2013; Stolker et al., 2016), HD 142527 (Avenhaus et al., 2014b; Canovas et al., 2013), HD 100453 (Wagner et al., 2015), HD 100546 (Boccaletti et al., 2013; Avenhaus et al., 2014a; Currie et al., 2014, 2015; Garufi et al., 2016), AK Sco (Janson et al., 2016), LkHα𝛼italic-alpha\alpha 330 (Akiyama et al., 2016), and V1247 Orionis (Ohta et al., 2016). The leading hypothesis for the origin of spiral arms is that they are excited by planets embedded in disks. Alternative explanations, such as gravitational instability(e.g., Pohl et al., 2015; Dong et al., 2015a; Kratter & Lodato, 2016) and radially bend shadows caused with finite photon travel time (Kama et al., 2016), have been investigated, but may not explain the observed arms so far.

Making quantitative predictions on the properties of the arm-driving planets is crucial to the study of planet formation. Because these planets are still located in their formation environment, their masses, orbits, and ages provide immediate constraints on planet formation models. The first attempt in this direction was made for SAO 206462 by Muto et al. (2012), in which the linear theory of planet-induced density waves (Equation 44 in Rafikov, 2002) was applied to fit the shape of the two observed arms to derive possible locations of two arm-driving planets. This linear theory fitting scheme was subsequently adopted in many other studies (e.g., Grady et al., 2013; Benisty et al., 2015; van der Marel et al., 2016; Janson et al., 2016). However, explaining observed spiral arms with linear wave theory is questionable due to the following three problems.

  1. 1.

    In the linear theory, one planet only excites one continuous spiral density wave (an inner part + an outer part, e.g., Fig. 4 in Ogilvie & Lubow 2002). In several cases a pair of nearly symmetric arms are present (e.g., MWC 758, SAO 206462, LkHα𝛼italic-alpha\alpha 330), demanding two planets at symmetric locations, which triggers dynamical stability issues.

  2. 2.

    In the linear theory, once waves are exited by planets at Lindblad resonance locations, they propagate as sound waves. Quantitative connections between the arm morphology to the planet mass, a key quantity to planet formation theory but very hard to measure, was first made by Fung & Dong (2015), who linked the symmetry of arms excited by one planet to the planet’s mass. What remains unknown is how the arm contrast (or strength) in scattered light, a direct observable, depends on the mass of an arm-driving planet. In general, the visibility of waves in scattered light is determined by the location and curvature of the disk surface at the waves’ locations (e.g., Takami et al., 2013), which are subsequently determined by both the wave amplitude in surface density, and the distribution of material in the vertical direction. The latter is determined by balancing gravity, gas pressure, and vertical kinematic support.

In this paper, we carry out 3D global numerical hydrodynamics and radiative transfer simulations (Section 2) to study the contrast of the arms as functions of planet mass and disk scale height (Section 3); based on which, we explore what kind of planets can drive visible arms, and comment on existing observed spiral arm systems (Section 4). In particular, we ask one specific question: is it possible to explain the observed arms with low mass planets each responsible for one arm as describable by the linear theory (Section 4.4)? This general topic has been previously touched by Rosotti et al. (2016) and Juhász et al. (2015), who found planets of tens of Earth mass are not able to drive detectable density waves. However, in both works the hydro calculations were done in 2D. Zhu et al. (2015) have shown that the vertical kinematics of the waves can significantly enhance their visibility in scattered light, thus 3D hydro simulations are needed. We further extend the Zhu et al. (2015) results to low mass planet regime (Section 3.1). Finally, we briefly discuss the prospects of spiral arm imaging with next generation telescopes in Section 4.6, before we summarize the main take aways in Section 5.

4.1 Inclined Disks

In this paper we focus on near-face-on systems. Inclined disks are more complicated for two reasons. First, the shape and strength of an arm can be dramatically affected by the disk inclination and the arm’s position angle (Dong et al., 2016a). Second, a global “azimuthally averaged” surface brightness does not exist, as the surface brightness at a given deprojected radius varies with azimuthal angle even without any planet, mainly due to the variation in scattering angle. In general this effect is difficult to correct, due to unknown dust properties. Therefore, Equation 11 becomes illy defined, and one can only define local feature contrast by comparing features with their immediate surrounding.
